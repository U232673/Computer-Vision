\section{Introduction}

Image feature matching is a key step in many computer vision tasks, such as
3D reconstruction or visual localization. The goal of this laboratory is to 
find reliable point correspondences between two or more images of the 
same scene, which may differ in viewpoint, scale, rotation, or illumination.

The most classical approach uses hand-crafted local feature descriptors. Among
them, SIFT~\cite{Lowe2004} is one of the most widely used methods. It detects
keypoints across scale space, assigns them an orientation, and describes them
with a 128-dimensional gradient histogram. This makes SIFT invariant to scale
and rotation, and partly robust to illumination changes. Since SIFT matches
always contain outliers, RANSAC is used on top of it to robustly estimate
homographies between image pairs.

Besides SIFT, many other descriptors have been developed. Binary descriptors
like ORB and BRISK are faster and suitable for real-time use. Methods like SURF
speed up detection with integral images, while FAST uses minimal computation.
However, all these methods share a key limitation: each descriptor is computed
locally, without any knowledge of the other image. This makes them unreliable
in textureless regions, repetitive patterns, or under motion blur.

To overcome this, LoFTR~\cite{Sun2021} uses a Transformer with self-attention 
to build dense correspondences at coarse resolution, then refines them to sub-pixel
accuracy. Its global receptive field allows matching even in low-texture regions
where classical detectors fail. Moreover, LightGlue~\cite{Lindenberger2023}
introduces a sparse deep matcher that works on top of any local detector. It
uses adaptive depth and width to stop early on easy pairs and prune poor
keypoints, achieving high accuracy at lower computational cost.

This report is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:Methods} describes the
theoretical background of each method. Section~\ref{sec:Experiments} presents
results for SIFT-based homography estimation and mosaicking, a comparison of
classical descriptors under synthetic transformations, and an evaluation of
LoFTR and LightGlue on challenging image pairs. Finally, Section~\ref{sec:Conclusion} 
draws conclusions and discusses the trade-offs between classical and data-driven pipelines.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:Methods}

This section details the implementation of the feature matching and homography estimation pipeline, covering classical local descriptors, modern Transformer-based architectures, and the robust RANSAC framework used for geometric verification.

\subsection{Descriptor Comparison Framework}
The script match\_comparison.m evaluates eight distinct algorithms: FAST, SIFT, SURF, KAZE, BRISK, ORB, Harris, and MSER. The evaluation focuses on the trade-off between match density and computational latency. In practice, the procedure implemented is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Detection and Extraction:} Each algorithm processes a pair of images (original and rotated) to identify keypoints and compute their respective feature vectors.
    \item \textbf{Matching:} Correspondences are established using a brute-force matcher. For binary descriptors (ORB, BRISK), Hamming distance is used, while $L_2$ norm is used for gradient-based descriptors (SIFT, SURF).
    \item \textbf{Timing:} The execution time is measured specifically for the extraction and matching phases to determine the efficiency of each method.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data-driven Matching: LoFTR and LightGlue}
Beyond classical methods, we explore "detector-free" and "deep matcher" paradigms using the \texttt{LoFTR} (Local Feature Transformer) and LightGlue architectures. 

\textbf{LoFTR Implementation:}
The core task of LoFTR is to establish dense matches without an explicit keypoint detection stage. The implementation follows these design choices:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Coarse-to-Fine Matching:} A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) extracts multi-scale feature maps. Transformers are applied at a coarse resolution to establish reliable region matches using self and cross-attention.
    \item \textbf{Attention Mechanism:} The Transformer layers allow the model to learn global context, making it robust in textureless regions where SIFT typically fails.
    \item \textbf{Sub-pixel Refinement:} Once coarse matches are found, they are refined to a finer resolution to achieve high localization accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{LightGlue Implementation:}
In contrast to LoFTR, \texttt{LightGlue} is used as a "deep matcher" for existing keypoints (such as those from SuperPoint or SIFT). It uses an adaptive Transformer that can stop computation early if a match is easy to find, significantly reducing inference time while maintaining state-of-the-art accuracy.

\subsection{Homography Estimation and RANSAC}
The robust estimation of the projective transformation $\mathbf{H}$ is handled by \texttt{ransacfithomography.m}. Because putative matches often contain outliers, we use the RANSAC algorithm to find the geometrically dominant plane.

\textbf{Key implementation details:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Normalisation:} Points are transformed via \texttt{normalise2dpts.m} so their centroid is at the origin and mean distance is $\sqrt{2}$. This ensures the Direct Linear Transform (DLT) is well-conditioned.
    \item \textbf{Symmetric Transfer Error:} To count inliers, we measure the distance in both images: $d(\mathbf{x}', \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x}) + d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{H}^{-1}\mathbf{x}')$. A pair is an inlier if this error is below \texttt{params.t}.
    \item \textbf{Pseudocode Overview:}
\end{itemize}

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{RANSAC for Homography Estimation}
\label{alg:ransac}
\begin{algorithmic}[1] % The [1] adds line numbers
\FOR{$i = 1$ \TO $maxIter$}
    \STATE $S \leftarrow$ Select 4 random matching pairs
    \STATE $H_{curr} \leftarrow$ Compute Homography using DLT on $S$
    \STATE $inliers \leftarrow$ Calculate symmetric transfer error for all pairs
    \IF{$count(inliers) > count(best\_inliers)$}
        \STATE $best\_inliers \leftarrow inliers$
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\STATE $H_{final} \leftarrow$ Refine $H$ using Least Squares on $best\_inliers$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Performance Metrics: Frobenius Norm}
To quantitatively verify accuracy against Ground Truth matrices, the code implements the Frobenius norm $E$. Both matrices are normalized such that the last element is 1:
\begin{equation}
E = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^3 \sum_{j=1}^3 (H_{i,j} - G_{i,j})^2}
\end{equation}
This metric allows us to evaluate the impact of image degradation (blur, lighting, and rotation) on the stability of the estimated homography.

\subsubsection{Real-World Applications: Image Mosaicing}
Beyond the controlled evaluation on the Bikes dataset, the framework was applied to a practical mosaicing task using two provided RGB images. This task required the integration of feature matching and homography estimation into a global coordinate mapping framework, as specified in the \textit{Mosaic SIFT} module.

\textbf{Implementation and Tasks:}
As required by the manual, the \texttt{main.m} script was completed by implementing the SIFT matching and homography estimation logic. The workflow followed a robust pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feature Correspondence:} SIFT descriptors were extracted and matched between the two captures to identify putative correspondences.
    \item \textbf{Robust Estimation:} The \texttt{ransacfithomography} function was integrated to compute the transformation matrix while effectively rejecting outliers.
    \item \textbf{Image Composition:} The estimated homography was used to warp the second image into the reference coordinate frame of the first, creating a unified mosaic.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Analysis of Results:}
The resulting mosaic (Fig.~\ref{fig:mosaic-results}) demonstrates the successful integration of the functions developed during the session. Despite the perspective shifts between the two captures, the RANSAC-refined homography ensured that architectural lines and textures remained continuous across the overlap region. This process effectively expanded the horizontal field of view, validating the precision of the matching algorithm in a real-world scenario.

\textbf{Implementation Details:}
Following the requirements in the \texttt{Mosaic SIFT} module, the \texttt{main.m} script was completed to automate the following pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SIFT Feature Extraction:} Keypoints and descriptors were extracted for both the reference and target images.
    \item \textbf{Putative Matching:} Matches were identified using the nearest neighbor distance ratio (NNDR) test.
    \item \textbf{Homography Estimation:} The \texttt{ransacfithomography} function was used to filter outliers, such as those caused by repetitive patterns in the scene architecture, and compute a refined $\mathbf{H}$ matrix.
    \item \textbf{Image Warping and Blending:} The target image was transformed into the reference coordinate frame, effectively doubling the horizontal field of view.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Descriptor Comparison}

The experiment uses the sunflower painting as the test image. Eight 
descriptors are evaluated across a range of synthetic transformations: 
scale $\in \{1.2, 1.6, 2.0\}$ and rotation $\in \{-60°,-40°,-20°,0°,20°,40°,60°\}$, 
resulting in 21 transformed images per descriptor.

Figure~\ref{fig:descriptor-results} shows the resulting heatmaps for number of
matches and computation time. Regarding match count, SIFT and SURF consistently
find the highest number of correspondences across both scale and rotation
changes, confirming their design objective of invariance to these transformations.
Moreover, KAZE also performs well, especially under rotation. In contrast, FAST, Harris,
and MSER produce significantly fewer matches, as they are primarily detectors
without strong descriptor invariance. On the other hand, ORB and BRISK
match counts degrades faster with increasing scale, as binary descriptors
are more sensitive to scale changes than gradient-based ones.

Regarding computation time, the binary descriptors ORB and BRISK are the
fastest. SIFT and SURF are considerably slower due to the cost of computing and comparing
floating-point gradient histograms. KAZE is the slowest overall, since it
builds a nonlinear scale space that is more expensive to construct. These
results show a clear trade-off: gradient-based descriptors are more robust but
slower, while binary descriptors are faster but less invariant.

\textbf{Indoor scene.}
Both methods produce a large number of matches. LoFTR finds 826 matches in
approximately 0.30\,s. LightGlue produces a comparable number and distributes
them well across the overlapping region. The feature distribution is correct in
both cases, covering textured areas such as the monitor, the papers, and the
desk edges. This confirms that a moderately textured indoor scene is a
favorable condition for both approaches.

\textbf{Motion blur.}
This pair is more challenging due to the camera motion that softens edges and
reduces gradient contrast. LoFTR finds 979 matches in around 0.29\,s.
LightGlue also produces a dense set of correspondences. However, some matches
are concentrated along the horizontal direction, which is consistent with the
direction of motion blur. Overall, both methods handle this case reasonably
well, though the match quality is lower compared to the indoor scene.

\textbf{Textureless scene.}
This is the most challenging pair. The images show a nearly uniform beige wall
with almost no local gradient structure. LoFTR finds 1060 matches in 0.29\,s.
This is because its Transformer-based design uses positional encoding and global
context, allowing it to differentiate between positions even in homogeneous
regions. In contrast, LightGlue produces only 88 matches in around 25.7\,s.
Since LightGlue relies on sparse keypoints from a detector, it struggles to
find repeatable interest points in flat regions, leading to very few and
spatially sparse correspondences. This case clearly highlights the advantage of
detector-free methods over detector-based ones in low-texture conditions.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:Conclusion}

This laboratory has studied feature matching from three perspectives: classical
SIFT-based homography estimation, a comparison of eight local descriptors, and
an evaluation of two modern deep learning methods.

In the first part, SIFT combined with RANSAC successfully estimates homographies
across all four datasets. The results show that match count decreases as image
degradation increases, which directly raises the Frobenius error. The Bikes and
Leuven datasets produce low errors even at high blur or illumination change,
because the dominant gradient structure is preserved. The Graf dataset is the
hardest, with errors exceeding 100 for the most extreme viewpoint changes,
where very few matches survive. This confirms that SIFT is robust to moderate
transformations but degrades under large viewpoint changes.

In the second part, the descriptor comparison reveals a clear trade-off between
robustness and speed. SIFT and SURF maintain a high number of matches across
scale and rotation, while binary descriptors such as ORB and BRISK are
significantly faster but less invariant. KAZE is the most computationally
expensive method. No descriptor is fully invariant to all transformations,
match counts drop consistently as scale or rotation increases.

In the third part, LoFTR and LightGlue both perform well on textured scenes.
However, the textureless pair reveals a fundamental difference. LoFTR finds over
1000 matches by leveraging global context through its Transformer architecture,
while LightGlue finds only 88 matches because it depends on a keypoint detector
that fails in uniform regions. This shows that detector-free methods are better
suited for challenging real-world scenes. The trade-off is computational cost:
LightGlue is much faster on easy pairs due to its adaptive early-stopping
mechanism, but this advantage disappears when the scene is difficult.

Overall, classical methods remain useful and efficient for standard conditions,
but data-driven approaches, especially detector-free ones like LoFTR, are
necessary when the scene lacks texture or contains motion blur.
